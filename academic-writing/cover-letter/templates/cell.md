# Cell (Press) Cover Letter

> Snapshot for the Cell Press family (Cell, Molecular Cell, Cancer Cell, Cell Reports, Cell Stem Cell, Immunity, Neuron, ...). Cell editors expect a mechanistic story with clear conceptual advance, not just a phenomenological observation.

## Tone

- Mechanism-forward. Cell editors discount letters that lead with a phenomenon without an underlying mechanism.
- Specific to the biological problem; avoid abstracted "high-impact" framing.

## Opening

- First sentence: the mechanism that was uncovered and why it answers an open question in the field.
- Sister journal hint: if the work is incremental, route to Molecular Cell or Cell Reports rather than Cell itself.

## Contribution Framing

- Two to three sentences naming the discovery, the mechanistic explanation, and the supporting evidence types (model, technique, perturbation).
- One quantitative anchor (effect size, knockout phenotype magnitude, ...).
- One sentence on why the mechanism is generalizable.

## Journal Fit

- Reference the specific Cell title and one or two recent papers that the present work extends.
- For Cell Reports: methodological completeness > paradigm-shift framing.

## Declarations (Required for Cell)

- Originality and exclusivity statements.
- Competing interests: explicit declaration.
- Ethics: IRB / IACUC numbers when applicable. Cell is strict on this.
- AI disclosure: state any generative-AI assistance to the manuscript or letter (ICMJE Jan 2026, Section V); Cell Press records LLM use in Methods as well. Grammar/spell-check is exempt.

## Conditional declarations (include only when relevant)

- Data deposition: GEO / SRA / PRIDE accession numbers go in the manuscript / submission system; mention in the letter only if the editor asks.
- Suggested / excluded reviewers: only when not collected by the submission form; not added by default (2025-26 tightening).

## Length

- Hard ceiling: 400 words.

## Common Desk-Reject Triggers

- Phenomenological observation pitched as mechanism.
- Missing IRB / IACUC numbers for studies that need them.
- Sister-journal mismatch (e.g., methodological paper sent to Cell instead of Cell Reports).
